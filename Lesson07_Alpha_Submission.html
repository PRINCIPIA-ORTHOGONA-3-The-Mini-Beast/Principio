✅ STAGE 1 — WARM-UP  ·  10 minutes
What is an alpha? (Discussion)

Before looking at the platform, discuss in pairs:

  1. Have you ever predicted something before it happened? How did you do it?
  2. What information do you think a computer could use to predict whether a stock price will go up or down?
  3. You already know Operator K (curvature/intensification). How do you think “applying an operator to price data” might work?
“An alpha is not a trade. It is a signal — a mathematical formula that ranks stocks from most likely to go up to most likely to go down. You are not buying or selling anything. You are predicting order.”
✅ STAGE 2 — VOCABULARY  ·  15 minutes
11 Key Terms — Copy into your notebook
TermMeaning in plain English
alphaa mathematical formula that predicts how stocks will be ranked by future return
universethe pool of stocks your formula looks at (e.g. the top 3,000 US stocks)
simulate / backtestto test your formula on historical data to see how it would have performed in the past
Sharpe ratioa measure of how good your returns are relative to risk; higher = better. Must be > 1.0 to pass.
fitnessWorldQuant’s combined quality score for your alpha. Must be > 1.0 to submit.
turnoverhow often your formula changes which stocks it holds. Very high turnover = expensive in real life.
operatora mathematical function you apply to data (rank, ts_rank, delta, log, abs…). Same word as in TO/TOGT.
neutralizationremoving a “market tilt” so your alpha is not just betting that all stocks go up together
decayhow quickly your formula’s signal fades. A decay of 4 means the signal is half as strong after 4 days.
submitto send your passing alpha to WorldQuant’s live pool, where it contributes to the IQC country score
aggregate scorethe total of all submitted alphas from one country (you saw this in Lesson 06)
✅ STAGE 3 — PLATFORM WALKTHROUGH  ·  30 minutes
Step-by-Step: From Opening the Simulator to Submitting
1

Open the simulator

Go to platform.worldquantbrain.com/simulate. Log in or create a free account. You will see a code editor on the left and settings on the right.

2

Choose your settings (Operator C — Compress)

In the right panel, set:

  • Region: USA
  • Universe: TOP3000 (the 3,000 most liquid US stocks)
  • Neutralization: Market
  • Decay: 4
  • Delay: 1
  • Truncation: 0.08

These settings define which stocks you are looking at and how your signal will be used. This is the Compress step: you are choosing your universe.

3

Write your alpha formula (Operator K — Curvature)

In the code editor, type your formula. Here is a simple starter alpha to use in class:

# Starter Alpha 1: Rank by recent volume change
rank(ts_delta(volume, 5))

What this does: ts_delta(volume, 5) calculates how much a stock’s trading volume has changed over the last 5 days. rank() converts that into a ranking from 0 to 1 across all stocks. Stocks with increasing volume rank higher.

# Starter Alpha 2: Rank by momentum (recent price trend)
rank(ts_mean(returns, 10))

What this does: returns is daily price change. ts_mean(..., 10) takes the 10-day average. rank() ranks all stocks by their recent trend. This is a simple momentum alpha.

# Starter Alpha 3: Contrarian (operators K and F together)
-rank(ts_mean(close/open - 1, 5))

The minus sign flips the signal: stocks that have been overperforming recently get a low rank. This bets on reversal (a common pattern in short-term trading).

4

Run the simulation (Operator F — Fold)

Click Simulate. The backtester will run your formula on historical data from the last several years. This takes 30–90 seconds. You will see a performance chart and a table of metrics.

This is the Fold step: the system is evaluating your formula. It will “fold” your idea against reality and show you what would have happened.

5

Read the output (did it pass?)

A typical simulator output looks like this:

Simulation complete.
Sharpe ratio: 1.42 ← Good. Must be > 1.0
Fitness: 1.18 ← Good. Must be > 1.0
Turnover: 0.71 ← Acceptable (0.0–0.9)
Returns: +0.082 ← 8.2% annualized
Drawdown: -0.14 ← Largest loss
Status: PASS — ready to submit

If fitness or Sharpe is below 1.0, the alpha fails. Try a different formula or settings and simulate again.

6

Submit (Operator U — Unfold)

If the alpha passes, click Submit. Your alpha enters the WorldQuant live pool. Within 24–48 hours, it will appear in your portfolio. Every accepted alpha you submit adds to the USA aggregate score in the IQC.

This is the Unfold step: your compressed, curved, folded idea now stabilises in the real world and generates a live signal.

✅ STAGE 4 — UNDERSTANDING THE METRICS  ·  15 minutes
Pass / Fail Thresholds — What the Numbers Mean
Sharpe Ratio
> 1.0
Return ÷ risk. How much profit per unit of volatility. A good alpha earns more than it risks.
Fitness
> 1.0
WorldQuant’s combined quality score. Includes Sharpe, uniqueness, and other factors.
Turnover
0.0–0.9
How often you change positions. Very high turnover is too costly in real trading.
MetricMeaningPass thresholdWhat to do if it fails
Sharpe ratioReturn per unit of risk> 1.0Try a longer lookback window or different operator
FitnessWorldQuant combined score> 1.0Improve Sharpe; try different neutralization (Market, Subindustry)
TurnoverHow often positions change0.01–0.9Increase decay (e.g. 4 → 6); use longer ts windows
ReturnsAnnualized profit> 0Flip the sign of your formula (add minus)
“The simulator is the Fold operator. It takes your idea and evaluates it against reality. If it passes, you submit — and that is the Unfold. Your idea becomes a live signal.”

✅ STAGE 6 — DISCUSSION  ·  10 minutes
Card A — G = U ∘ F ∘ K ∘ C in Finance

We have used these operators since Lesson 01. In the Cajueiro lesson, C was “compression toward a stable form.” In the alpha simulation, C is “choose your universe.”

Is this the same idea or a different one? What does it mean to say that the same mathematical structure appears in biology, physics, language learning, and finance?

Card B — The USA Leaderboard

In Lesson 06, the USA had 2,331 participants and an aggregate score of 674,966 — 9th place. China had 24,454 participants and a score of 53,568,436 — 1st place.

What would happen to the USA score if every student in this classroom submitted one passing alpha today? Do the math together. What does this tell you about the power of collective action?

Teacher Notes — Students do not need finance experience. Frame this entirely as “applying mathematical operators to data” — which is exactly what they have been doing in TO/TOGT since Lesson 01.

Accounts: WorldQuant Brain accounts are free. Create yours in advance and walk through the simulator with students projected on screen. For students who cannot create accounts, the walkthrough as a demonstration still covers all learning objectives.

Starter alphas: All three starter formulas are simple enough to type in 30 seconds. Starter 1 (volume momentum) and Starter 3 (contrarian) tend to perform best in recent years. Results vary by simulation date — show students that the machine tells them honestly when it fails.

IQC connection: At the start of Stage 6, look up the current USA position on the live leaderboard together. If any student submits a passing alpha during class, refresh the leaderboard at the end to see if the score has moved.

For stronger students: Explore the Operators library on the platform (rank, ts_rank, ts_corr, group_rank, adv20…). Ask: which of these corresponds to C? Which to K? Which to F?
